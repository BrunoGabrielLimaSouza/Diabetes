What is EDA?

EDA, or Exploratory Data Analysis, is the essential first step in any data project. It helps us deeply understand the data before applying models or drawing conclusions.

During EDA, we use statistics and visualizations to identify patterns, detect missing or extreme values, and understand relationships between variables. This makes our analysis more reliable and well-founded.

“Knowing your data is the first step to making good decisions with it.”

The heatmap shows strong correlations between glucose, BMI and age with the occurrence of diabetes.

Most of the data belongs to non-diabetic patients, indicating an unbalanced dataset.

The gender distribution is relatively balanced, useful for comparative risk analysis.

The boxplot highlights extreme BMI and glucose values, indicating possible outliers.

People between 40 and 60 years old represent the most affected group, according to the data.

Diabetic individuals tend to have higher BMI values compared to non-diabetics. However, the peak near BMI 25 for both groups suggests a general overweight population.

Women represent the largest group among both diabetic and non-diabetic individuals. Men follow, while the “Other” category is statistically insignificant. This may reflect sample collection bias or real-world trends.

Most participants either never smoked or do not currently smoke. Interestingly, the diabetic proportion is visible across all smoking categories, with a slightly higher frequency among former and occasional smokers.

Diabetic individuals are more concentrated at higher blood glucose levels, especially above 150 mg/dL. Non-diabetics show a more balanced distribution with noticeable peaks around normal fasting values (100–130 mg/dL).

Diabetic individuals show higher HbA1c levels, commonly above 6.5, which aligns with clinical diagnostic thresholds. Non-diabetics cluster around lower values, reinforcing HbA1c as a key biomarker for diabetes diagnosis.

Based on analyses of the Diabetes Prediction Dataset.
